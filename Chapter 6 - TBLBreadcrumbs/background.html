Understanding Cybersecurity

Companies and media often toss the word “cybersecurity” around, and there are several valid ways it can be defined. A simplistic way to define cybersecurity is that it is the suite of technologies, methodologies, and practices that are designed to safeguard an individual's or organization's assets from potential threats in the digital realm. These assets can include anything that is considered to have value, ranging from computer systems, networks, and software applications to sensitive data and information. While the word cybersecurity can be viewed as a blanket term that encompasses various aspects within the IT security landscape, understanding the basic risks and protective strategies is paramount in today's interconnected digital ecosystem. Malicious actors constantly seek vulnerabilities to exploit, potentially causing devastating harm to individuals, universities, businesses, and even nations. As our reliance on digital platforms grows, both individually and institutionally, so does the reason to fortify our cybersecurity.

After establishing a general definition of cybersecurity, it is essential to examine its core principles. These principles are confidentiality, integrity, and availability – often referred to collectively as the “CIA Triad”. Confidentiality ensures that sensitive information is accessible only to those who have authorized access. Integrity involves maintaining the accuracy and reliability of data. Finally, availability is about ensuring that information and resources are accessible to authorized users when needed.

Cybersecurity in Universities

When applied to universities, these principles of the CIA triad are combined to protect many different types of data. These data types include the personal information of students and staff, academic records, graduate research, internal communications, and digital resources such as WebCourses and the student portal. Each data type demands specific cybersecurity attention. For instance, the personal information of students and faculty is safeguarded through confidentiality, ensuring authorized access only and preventing breaches of privacy. Meanwhile, the integrity of academic records and research data forms the backbone of every university, so they must be accurate and reliable at all times. Lastly, the operation of educational and administrative activities relies on the continuous availability of digital resources like the ones previously mentioned. Together, these principles of confidentiality, integrity, and availability form a critical framework for cybersecurity in any industry – especially in education. They not only ensure the protection of diverse and sensitive data but also uphold the integrity and efficiency of digital ecosystems.

General Cybersecurity Technologies

The dynamic landscape of cyber threats requires a multifaceted cybersecurity approach that constantly adapts and evolves. To address these challenges, universities have or must adopt a wide range of general cybersecurity technologies that integrate with either new or pre-existing IT infrastructure.

Firewalls and Intrusion Prevention Systems

Firewalls are the primary line of defense in IT networking systems; their job is to monitor incoming and outgoing network traffic based on rules, and if certain traffic breaks those rules, it gets blocked. For example, the University of Central Florida decided that TikTok should not be able to have any communication with their university network. So, a firewall rule was set that blocks any outgoing traffic from the UCF network to any TikTok domain and blocks incoming traffic from any TikTok domain to a device on the UCF network. Firewalls have the power to do things like this, but they effectively block unauthorized access and prevent malicious traffic from entering the network. On the other hand, Intrusion Prevention Systems (IPS) have a different purpose. Their job is to actively analyze network traffic to detect and prevent vulnerability exploits. These systems are great for identifying attacks before they cause any harm, so if an attacker can infiltrate the university network, they can’t easily send malicious traffic without being detected.

Antivirus and Anti-Malware Software

Universities can and should deploy antivirus and anti-malware solutions across their networks. These types of software are designed to detect malicious programs, isolate them, and eliminate them from devices. They must be regularly updated and regularly scan systems to ensure effective protection against the latest threats, but are sometimes bypassable depending on the type of malware, so they should not be fully relied on. Windows computers are typically used in university environments, and they come with a built-in antivirus software called Microsoft Defender. It is critical that Microsoft Defender is turned on and up to date on all university and personal Windows computers to protect against malware.

Data Encryption

In addition to antivirus software, encryption is used to protect sensitive data when it is sitting on a server or in transit across a network. By encrypting data-at-rest such as student records, research data, and financial information, universities can ensure that information is unreadable even if a breach occurs – stolen data has no value if you can’t read it. It is also paramount to encrypt data as it is in transit. Otherwise, an attacker can sit between two devices communicating, and “sniff” the traffic to read the data being transmitted. A common way that data is encrypted in transit is by using a virtual private network or VPN. A VPN creates a secure connection for remote access and is essential for faculty and students accessing university resources from off-campus.

Two-Factor Authentication (2FA or MFA)

Two-factor authentication is increasing in popularity across every industry, including education. It is a concrete way to enhance the security of user accounts, especially those with access to critical systems. 2FA adds an extra layer of security to user accounts by requiring a second form of identification after logging in with a password. This can be a one-time code sent to a smartphone upon logging in, a fingerprint, or a physical security key via USB. There are many forms of 2FA, and it is an excellent way to protect user accounts from being compromised. An attacker may be able to determine a password, but the extra security layer connected to something only you own is something very difficult to bypass.

Cybersecurity Awareness and Training

Aside from these few critical technologies used to protect university networks and devices, cybersecurity and training programs are of the utmost importance. Human error is the number one cause of successful cybersecurity attacks, so educating students and faculty about potential cyber threats, safe computing practices, and response procedures is paramount in protecting university environments.

The combination of these technologies with a strong culture of cybersecurity awareness enables universities to build a strong defense against cyber threats. However, cyber attacks still occur and many are successful. These basic defenses, while necessary, are not one hundred percent effective at mitigating cyber risk. This underscores the crucial aspect of cybersecurity in any setting: the need for an in-depth understanding of the evolving threats. Recognizing the limitations of current defenses is not just about identifying gaps but also about understanding the specific challenges and vectors that attackers exploit.
