University Threats

Having established the foundational principles of cybersecurity and their application within university environments, it is important to identify and understand the common cyber threats that universities face. These institutions are unique targets for cybercriminals; they hold vast amounts of sensitive data, including intellectual property from research projects, extensive personal records of students and staff, and financial information.

Social Engineering (Phishing)

Figure 7-4: Flowchart depicting how attackers infiltrate network.

Threats can come from many directions. One form of threat is called social engineering. Social engineering is a branch of hacking centering around obtaining information or encouraging employees to take actions they otherwise should not – in other words, “human hacking”. A common example of social engineering is phishing attacks, where attackers disguise themselves as trustworthy entities to trick university students and employees into sharing confidential information. Attackers may even spend time studying their targets, where they work, what their interests are, and what their problems are; this information is used to craft highly personalized and convincing messages. This more targeted form of phishing, known as spear phishing, often involves emails that appear to come from trusted senders such as university administrators, fellow employees, fellow students, or even the financial aid office. The goal is to create a sense of trust or urgency and convince the recipient to click on malicious links, reveal confidential information, or download compromised attachments. These attacks are not only common but also increasingly sophisticated, making them a huge concern for university cybersecurity teams. According to Cloudflare’s 2023 Phishing Report, an estimated 90% of all successful cyber attacks start with phishing [1]. It is the golden first step for attackers aiming to breach university cybersecurity defenses. Once they gain access through a successful phishing attempt, they can exploit the network and further their malicious objectives from a better position. It’s easier to avoid detection, gain access, and cause issues as a student or employee than as an outsider.

Malware and Ransomware

Figure 7-4: Malware attacks - Line graph showing malware attacks by industry in 2022.

Aside from phishing, universities also face significant threats from malware. Malware, or malicious software, refers to various types of software designed to harm or exploit any device, service, or network. The most common forms of malware include viruses, worms, and trojans – each of which operates differently. Viruses operate by attaching themselves to clean files within a computer system. Once attached, they spread throughout the system, corrupting data and disrupting functionality as they replicate. Unlike viruses, which rely on a trigger for activation, worms are more independent. Worms can infect entire networks when replicating themselves, not just a single system. They are essentially viruses with the capability to move autonomously across machines in a network, causing massive damage without the need for a trigger or user intervention. Lastly, trojans are a form of malicious software that is disguised as legitimate software to deceive users into loading and executing the malware on their systems. Trojans are particularly dangerous because they exploit user trust. Each of these malicious programs is designed to infiltrate and damage computer systems, steal data, or disrupt operations.

Malware can be used to compromise university research data or hijack administrative systems, and the education industry is a prime target. Between 2021 and 2022, SonicWall’s Cyber Threat Report showed that malware attacks against higher education institutions increased by 26% [2].

Another prevalent threat is ransomware, a type of malware that encrypts data and demands a ransom for its release. Ransomware attacks not only cripple university operations but also pose a serious threat to the privacy and security of sensitive information. It can make university data inaccessible and cause serious financial implications, not just in terms of the ransom demanded but also due to the costs associated with system recovery and data loss. Moreover, ransomware attacks often lead to a loss of trust in an institution's ability to safeguard sensitive information, which can have lasting reputational damage. Like malware attacks, ransomware attacks pose a significant threat to the education industry and universities. From 2018 to mid-September 2023, 561 ransomware attacks were confirmed on schools and universities, resulting in a breach of 6.7 million records and an estimated downtime cost of about $53.4 billion [3].

Vulnerable Networks and Programs

One of the most challenging issues in university cybersecurity is the weakness of their massive computer networks. Universities typically have vast networks that support a wide range of academic and administrative activities. These networks not only connect the campus computers and servers but also the thousands of devices brought in by students and staff, including smartphones, tablets, and laptops. In addition, each semester brings in a new wave of students each with their own devices that can be potential entry points for cyber threats. This dynamic nature of university environments along with the variety and volume of devices creates a complex network that can be difficult to monitor and secure.

The complexity of these networks is further increased due to the fact that many universities still rely on legacy systems – systems that are old or outdated and may not be equipped to handle modern cyber threats. These legacy systems often have known vulnerabilities that are easy targets for hackers, and it’s not always easy to upgrade these systems without breaking existing network functions and spending copious amounts of money. At the same time, missing even a single software update on one system can make a university more vulnerable to an attack [4]. Universities must find a balance between managing their existing networks and devices and upgrading them accordingly to prevent vulnerabilities – a task that is both complex and resource-intensive.
